When the Belt Fights Back

The economy fights back. Mined ships call for help, pirate bases hunt them, and militia answer when you bleed, if someone can afford to come.

A belt is not just rock and yield. It is a place where people are trying to make a living, and other people are trying to take it from them — and once you know how to read that, a quiet mining run stops being scenery and starts being a fight you can walk into on either side.

A cry on the open channel

When a prospector working a belt takes fire, it does not just die quietly. It broadcasts — a distress call on the open channel, flagged with its faction and its position. That call is the whole engine of what happens next. Everything downstream is a reaction to a working ship saying I am being killed out here, is anyone coming?

It calls once and then holds its tongue for a beat, even under a hail of fire — one call per skirmish, not one per hit — so the channel carries a warning, not a scream.

The base that hunts

Some belts have a pirate bastion dug into the edge — you’ll read it as a hollowed asteroid welded with a scavenged reactor spine, amber and mean (see Battle Stations). It is not just a gun emplacement sitting there. It is a predator’s den: on its own clock it launches hunters — fast raider hulls — and points them at the gather ships working the ore road.

A hunter prefers prey. Put a defenceless prospector and an armed warship in front of one and it goes for the prospector every time, because a base like that isn’t hunting for a fair fight — it’s hunting for cargo and for the message that the belt belongs to the raiders. A base keeps only so many hunters in the field at once, and when the belt is picked clean of prey, it calls them home.

Who answers, and whether they can

Here is the part that makes it an ecology and not just a spawner: the answer to a distress call is not free.

A militia watch station standing over a belt banks the ore its prospectors haul in — every unloaded hold is materials in the station’s stores. When a distress call comes in from its own people or a friendly flag, the station spends those materials to scramble a defender and send it toward the trouble. The defender flies the militia’s colors, engages the raiders on sight, and stands down once the shooting stops.

But it can only come if the station can afford it. A defender costs materials, and a station whose stores are empty cannot pay — so a starved watch stays silent, and the call goes unanswered. That is the hard rule of it: space answers when you bleed, but only if someone can afford to come. Strip a belt of the miners who fund the defense, or catch a station after a bad stretch, and you’ll hear a prospector calling into a channel where no one is left to reply.

Reading it, and using it

  • A belt with a pirate bastion is a live hunting ground — expect raiders on the ore road, not a still life.
  • The prospectors working it are prey. Their distress calls are the tripwire; if you’re hostile to the raiders, following the calls puts you where the fight is.
  • The militia’s ability to defend rises and falls with the belt’s own economy. A well-worked belt funds its own guardians. A dead one defends no one.

You can push on any thread of that. Thin the miners and the militia goes broke. Break the bastion (see Battle Stations — beat its shield flat and its guns go dark) and the hunts stop for a while. Or just fly in on the raiders’ side and find out how fast a lone prospector’s call goes cold.
