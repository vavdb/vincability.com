Termitus

Termitus nest ships consume raw materials and build patient industrial expansion.

Builders. Hive-minded, insectoid, and industrially voracious. Where the Termitus appear, structures follow: orbital spires, processor arrays, and gate anchors assembled from raw materials in weeks. They do not explore. They expand.

A Termitus nest ship is a living factory: it consumes asteroids, refines them internally, and extrudes finished components from its hull. Their ships are chitinous, faceted, and warm — interior temperature is kept at a humidity most species find unbearable.

Behaviour

Termitus do not negotiate for territory. If a system contains resources they need and no Termitus presence, they will seed a nest. Nests grow. If the nest encounters resistance — sapient colonies, defended trade routes — it does not attack. It requests access via a biochemical signal that translates, roughly, to permission-to-harvest. If denied? It waits. Termitus are patient. They remember denial. And they grow elsewhere until the denial can be revisited.

This patience makes them excellent long-term trading partners and terrible short-term enemies. A Termitus queen does not forget a slight; she simply outlives it.

Relationship to the Ancients

Termitus treat Ancient ruins the way a forest treats a fallen log: as material to be broken down and reformed. They do not worship the structures. They use them. A Termitus-hulled gate anchor is recognisable by its rippled, organic surface — grown, not forged, and twice as durable as any successor equivalent.

In the field

Encountering Termitus outside their space is rare. Encountering their structures is routine: every system with dense resources eventually hosts a Termitus nest, and a nest left alone is a nest that produces trade goods, refined fuel, and stable gate anchors.
