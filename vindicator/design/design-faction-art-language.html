Faction art language — production implementation

Design note — systems rationale, not player-facing codex text.

Status: Internal production art bible; forward design, not shipped codex.

System spine

Hull = faction body · Hardpoints = neutral sockets · Weapon = modular item (template ± module wrap) · FX/art = item family, recolorable · Default loadout ≠ unique inseparable gun.

Start at the visual-combat spine. This bible owns silhouette, palette, materials, wear, hull and station grammar, and reveal-safe labels. It does not own canvas/export/filter rules, per-weapon DPS, ammo economy, or runtime IDs. Technical presentation lives in the art style guide; modular hardpoints and art-to-template mapping live in weapons and modules; resource gates live in munitions; and hull IDs, ladders, proxy mappings, and default loadouts live in fleet content.

This document defines the visual grammar, asset boundaries, and first production rosters for the factions that need a coherent art language now. It is allowed to contain spoilers so artists understand why forms relate. It does not change gameplay, shipped art, or player-facing lore by itself.

Player-facing art labels, captions, codex copy, and marketing must preserve the reveal ladder in the story outline and story design bible. In particular, they must not disclose that humanity and the Ancients are the same civilization, or identify the Unbound’s condition as the Fold-Plague, before those truths are revealed in play. Internal asset names may be explicit; player-visible names may not.

This bible specializes faction identity within the shared technical rules in the art style guide. Existing pack art and assignments are inventoried in the asset inventory; they are proxies and references, not binding final silhouettes. Current hull-animation limitations are recorded in the Foozle animation survey. Player-facing hull roles remain defined by Ships & Hull Classes.

Shared production contract

Presentation track

The final direction is high-resolution cel hull and structure art with broad, clean material planes, controlled contour antialiasing, and shapes that remain readable when downscaled. Energy FX are separate and may use soft alpha, subpixel motion, and restrained bloom. Existing low-resolution pixel packs are runtime proxies during the transition, not a second final style or a mandate for new art.

Gameplay art remains strict top-down and nose-up. Black is permitted only on concept review plates; production bases and overlays use transparent alpha with adequate padding. Master sizes, delivery sizes, filtering, import/schema changes, camera scale, animation layout, and legacy pixel compatibility are owned by the art style guide, not repeated in this faction bible.

Base hull and effect separation

The clean base hull contains structure only. It may include dark engine nozzles, closed apertures, sockets, physical shield emitters, and unlit weapon mounts. It must not contain:

  • engine flame, exhaust, heat shimmer, or glow outside the silhouette;
  • an active shield, shield halo, or impact flash;
  • weapon fire, beam, projectile, muzzle flash, or charge effect;
  • a baked shadow or drop shadow;
  • destruction debris, explosion, smoke, or damage flash.

Engines, shields, weapons, and destruction are separate aligned assets or engine-side effects. This lets thrust respond to motion, shields respond to impacts, weapons originate from real hardpoints, and wreck states remain authorable rather than painted into the living hull.

Modular weapons

Weapons are modular retrofit items: the same mechanical template may ship built into a craft or wrapped as a grid module. Hull art never bakes permanent unique guns as the only way to express faction identity; an optional role graft, such as one oversized Korrathi launcher integrated as a chassis feature, is the narrow exception. Artists author neutral hardpoint anchors, then gun sprites and separate FX attach at runtime. Default NPC loadouts are content choices over modules and templates, not a second art pipeline. Loot, salvage, shops, and fabrication can move the same recolorable visual kit across factions: ownership comes from hull anatomy and markings, not from declaring that only one faction may use a given bolt. See weapons and modules for item families and mapping.

The current animated-hull path and visual.base overlay path are mutually exclusive. A template that adopts idle/move/attack hull-body animation currently loses the visual.base overlay cluster used for mounts, engine flame, shield, and destruction. Therefore art deliverable readiness is not wiring readiness. Do not merge body animation and overlay layers into one compromise sheet; hold the complete source cluster until the schema work described in the animation survey lands.

Readability gates

Every approved asset must pass all of these checks:

  1. Solid-black silhouette at intended gameplay footprint: faction and front/back read without color or markings.
  2. Flat-color thumbnail: role and scale class read at actual gameplay scale.
  3. Dark-space plate: dark hull materials remain separated from the background.
  4. Mixed-fleet plate: the asset cannot be mistaken for another detailed faction.
  5. Motion plate: thrust direction, turn center, shield footprint, and weapon origin remain unambiguous.

No cockpit, shuttle bay, major weapon, engine anchor, or navigation-critical edge may depend on detail lost at gameplay scale. A concept that works only enlarged is not ready for sprite production.

Source layers, pivots, anchors, and hardpoints

Keep editable source files with named layers for base hull, panel groups, cockpit/glass, faction markings, emissives, damage, engine mounts, shield emitters, and each movable weapon. Provide a flattened clean-base PNG only after the layer file is archived.

Every asset gets an anchor sheet that states:

  • tile dimensions, nose direction, center pivot, and opaque bounds;
  • engine/exhaust anchors and the expected thrust vector;
  • weapon hardpoints, muzzle points, firing arcs, and turret pivots;
  • docking, bay, tow, cargo, gate-throat, or drone-launch anchors as applicable;
  • shield center and radius or the aligned shield-strip frame;
  • detachable pieces, damage seams, and destruction origin.

Pivots stay stable across base, variants, wear tiers, and all animation frames. Hardpoints belong to functional geometry, not convenient empty space.

Ancient / Vindicator

Design promise

Ancient hardware is retro-modern optimistic industrial design: the confident future imagined from the 1960s through the 1980s, realized as genuinely advanced craft. It is elegant, tactile, maintainable, and humane rather than mystical. Eons of abandonment interrupt that optimism without erasing it. Vindicator craft are restored or reconfigured members of the same family, not a separate style.

The permanent body chain is shuttle ⊂ Vindicator ⊂ host. Art must never depict Vann transferring a loose core between disposable bodies or abandoning the shuttle. The shuttle remains visibly nested, protected, or structurally implied inside every larger body even when the external silhouette changes.

Silhouettes and shape vocabulary

  • Long triangular wedges with clear pointed noses and composed bilateral weight.
  • The ring is the Ancient structural signature, and it is the fold drive (see codex travel-folding.md and ships.md). A concentric ring carries the wedge rather than decorating it: the hull passes through it, the ring takes load, and the craft reads as built around that ring outward. It is the same technology as a stargate at ship scale, so the stargate is the ring alone. A hull with a ring can fold; the shuttle has none and cannot. When an Ancient hull folds, the wormhole throat anchors through the ring, not off the nose, so keep the ring clear as an aperture the effect layer can drive. Small craft may carry none, fold-capable craft carry one, and the largest repeat them concentrically.
  • Surfaces are sleek and flowing but strictly geometric. Smooth planes meet at clean machined cuts, curvature is swept and continuous, and every transition looks turned or milled. This is not biomorphic softness: Blackwater hulls flow organically, Ancient hulls flow because someone machined the curve.
  • Rounded rectangles, capsule windows, broad shoulder plates, and smooth wing roots soften the wedge.
  • Negative space is deliberate: forked engine shoulders, recessed bays, the open volume framed inside a ring, or a service notch must look engineered rather than broken at random.
  • Stations and gates scale the same grammar into concentric, modular structures with repeating ribs and legible access paths.
  • The silhouette should feel fast where it is a ship and patiently permanent where it is infrastructure.

Monumental infrastructure grammar

The Stargate is the reference asset for Ancient monumental infrastructure. Build the first read from a few thick concentric load-bearing bands, deep charcoal recesses, and a disciplined rhythm of large radial anchors. The construction is modular and exact, but not ornamental: repeated modules explain assembly and force transfer before any surface detail is added. Use broad stepped profiles, regular seams, and restrained inset channels so the silhouette survives at gameplay scale.

Gates carry the Ancient palette at its coolest. Dark gunmetal, slate, and blue-grey hold the mass, pale ceramic sits on structural faces, edge plates, and socket blocks, and cyan is the only light present. Dormant infrastructure earns no orange service marks and no green confirmation, because nothing has been restored. Station-scale Ancient machinery follows the same weighting and keeps the ordered panel logic of smaller Ancient craft.

Avoid mystical filigree, decorative glyph belts, gold trim, spikes, random greeble fog, and disconnected floating ornaments. Ancient infrastructure is advanced human engineering, not a ritual object. It is free-floating orbital machinery: never add a ground pedestal, architectural foot, or visual support that implies a planetary installation unless the asset’s role explicitly calls for one.

Palette

Use a compact family built around:

  • pale blue-grey #91A8AD and shadow #526D75;
  • charcoal internals #202A30 and outline #151C21;
  • warm ivory #D9D3BF and highlight #F2ECD7;
  • safety orange #D86A2B with light accent #F3A34F;
  • cyan glass/energy #45C9D5 with hot core #B7F5F2;
  • small green player confirmation lights #69C46B.

Blue-grey and charcoal carry the mass. Ancient hulls are cool slate ceramic, not cream: warm ivory is an accent reserved for structural faces, edge plates, and socket blocks, and it never becomes the body. The shipped stargate rings and the Vindicator are the reference for this weighting.

Orange identifies service, hazard, and rescue functions. Cyan identifies active Ancient systems. Green is a sparse confirmation state on player-controlled or successfully restored hardware, never the dominant hull color.

Restoration reads through light, not repaint. The base hull is slate and cyan at every stage, dormant or restored. Orange and green never join the hull mass: they are small functional marks and lights that appear when a system is in service or under player control, and they read as state layered onto the craft rather than as a change of livery.

Materials and surfaces

Primary shells are satin ceramic composite over anodized structural metal. Dark rubber seals, thick cyan-tinted glass, brushed access rails, and clean insulated cable bundles provide material contrast. Broad surfaces carry the read; tiny greebles do not. Surviving internals remain unusually ordered.

Construction and panel logic

Panel seams follow maintenance access and load paths. Corners are radiused, fastener spacing is regular, and orange alignment marks explain how a panel opens. Do not scatter decorative seams across every plane.

Use three authored wear tiers:

  • Intact: all primary panels present; tiny age variation only.
  • Service-worn: one mismatched replacement, faded markings, or opened service hatch, but the craft is fully legible and functional.
  • Derelict: most old field objects receive exactly one deliberate missing panel exposing orderly ribs, a cyan/orange service bus, ceramic-insulated cabling, or an empty module socket.

The missing panel is selected by the artist, not randomized per spawn. It never hides a cockpit, shuttle bay, front/back cue, weapon or hardpoint, engine/FX anchor, or required animation seam. Not every object must lose the same panel, and wear must never collapse all Ancient assets into one universal damaged state. The Vindicator keeps one repaired scar or mismatched replacement panel as history, not as an open fatal wound.

Wear and damage

Eons produce bleaching, ceramic edge chips, hairline fractures, seal decay, faded alignment ink, and darkened exposed structure. Avoid ordinary orange rust, random grime clouds, or freshly torn scrapyard texture. Combat damage is sharper: localized impact craters, fractured ceramic, cleanly exposed ribs, and failing cyan buses. Derelict and combat-damaged layers must remain distinct.

Lighting and emissives

Cyan is the normal operational light. Orange marks warnings and service states; green confirms player ownership, handshake, or restored control. On ordinary hardware, emissives are small, crisp, and tied to apertures or light pipes. Monumental infrastructure may expand that same language into broad, contained energy apertures and routed ring channels. Complete-ring atlas phases may carry the routed surface current while a separate shader owns the aperture field, but neither may alter the underlying physical construction or become undirected neon strips or soft bloom painted into the dormant structure.

Motion and VFX behavior

Engines start with controlled cyan-white exhaust and steady synchronized pulses. Large systems wake in ordered sequences from center to edge. Shields assemble as precise pale-cyan facets or rings. Weapons charge cleanly at visible hardpoints. Ancient failure is restrained: a missed pulse, dark sector, or stuttering bus is more characteristic than wild sparking everywhere.

Weapons and VFX verb

Charge gathers cleanly at an authored hardpoint around a narrow white-hot core and controlled cyan halo, followed by a restrained muzzle response. Ancient weapons never read as scrap bolts or improvised barrels. Reuse tintable shared weapon and FX families through the modular weapon contract, not a unique sheet per hull.

Scale and readability

The shuttle reads as a compact medical/rescue craft; the Vindicator adds military shoulders, cradle handshake hardware, and stronger longitudinal authority without erasing the shuttle’s body. Larger objects repeat recognizable service openings, ribs, and orange alignment marks at coarser scales. At gameplay scale, preserve the nose, cockpit, shoulder masses, and aft nozzles before any panel detail.

Markings

Use non-readable geometric rescue bands, serial blocks, alignment ticks, docking chevrons, and colored service zones. Ancient typography may exist in close-up environment art but must not become legible player-facing proof before the story allows it. The Vindicator may carry one restrained green control mark.

Forbidden traits

  • No mystical filigree, fantasy runes, floating ornaments, or inexplicable gold.
  • No disposable core pod, abandoned shuttle, or visible body-transfer sequence.
  • No random missing-panel selection or damage that breaks functional anchors.
  • No ordinary rust bucket treatment, dense greebles, or universal wreck texture.
  • No baked engine flame, shield, weapon fire, shadow, or destruction.

Production and variant rules

Build one clean master per chassis, then derive function variants through approved module, marking, and wear layers. Preserve family proportions and anchors between tiers. Ancient ruins use derelict layers; restored player hardware uses intact or service-worn layers. A variant must change role readability, not merely hue.

Ancient-Stargate — MVP / Act I

  • Silhouette: a free-floating ring-on-ring station: one large sender ring and one smaller receiver ring overlapping its lower-right foreground. The receiver’s centre sits on the middle of the sender’s structural band. Keep both rings complete and independently readable; no pedestal, connecting spine, double-funnel throat, or symmetrical figure-eight.
  • Role/read: monumental lane infrastructure, older and more exact than any faction-built station.
  • Construction/materials: thick segmented gunmetal and slate bands, charcoal recesses, restrained warm ceramic faces, and exactly eight repeated radial anchors. Detail density stays medium-low: broad engineered modules first, regular seams and shallow channels second, no filigree or greeble fog.
  • State read: sender and receiver each use complete-ring atlas phases whose metal structure is identical in every power and repair state. Active phases add routed surface energy; offline, dormant, and broken phases go dark and still. They never gain cracks, missing pieces, rubble, or damaged geometry.
  • Animation/VFX: sender and receiver are independently controlled for all four on/off combinations. Deep blue-cyan sender energy travels inward from its eight emitters toward the aperture; receiver energy travels outward from its aperture toward its eight emitters. The two metal rings slowly counter-rotate, while both shader-driven aperture fields remain stationary in world space. Keep the vortex, glow, and event accents out of the physical ring artwork.

Ancient-R&D-Station-and-Vault — Act III / post-game

  • Silhouette: layered research station wrapped around a protected geometric vault, with a clear approach axis and human-scale inner modules.
  • Role/read: forbidden deep infrastructure and narrative destination, not a generic orbital city. Act II needs only an intel or schematic representation; it does not require a production station asset.
  • Distinctive features: repeated laboratory pods, disciplined missing-panel exposure, sealed cyan core, and architecture that echoes Vindicator geometry.
  • Animation/VFX: staged power-up sectors, door and lock anchors, local shield fields, separate data/energy effects, and authored intact/derelict states.

Ancient-Cradle-Host — future

  • Silhouette: massive carrier/body shell whose central cradle unmistakably contains the complete Vindicator, preserving the nesting chain.
  • Role/read: strategic host body and mobile fortress, never a replacement that makes the inner craft disposable.
  • Distinctive features: protected insertion geometry, repeated Ancient ribs, broad host-scale shoulders, and one retained historical repair region.
  • Animation/VFX: cradle lock sequence, host engines and shields, multi-scale weapon anchors, detachable damage pieces, and no core-transfer imagery.

Korrathi

Design promise

Korrathi ships are scavenger weapons: stolen Ancient technology visibly grafted onto hulls whose crews understand its immediate use but not its original system. They should look dangerous while parked—red, jagged, forward-hungry, and one improvised upgrade away from structural failure. They are clans, not a formal navy.

Silhouettes and shape vocabulary

  • Forward hooks, saw teeth, spear points, split noses, and raked shoulders.
  • Deliberate asymmetry in grafts and armor masses over a flight-stable core.
  • Oversized guns, engines, clamps, or salvaged Ancient modules distort the host.
  • Aft structures taper or look under-supported; mass and threat point forward.

Palette

  • blood red #A92E32 and scarlet highlight #E04B3F;
  • near-black plating #1D2024 and outline #111316;
  • burnt steel #55545A and hot edge #858087;
  • dirty bone #B7AA8B;
  • unstable amber #F09A32 and hot core #FFD071.

Red is clan threat/ownership; amber is heat, power, and stolen-system activity. Dirty bone may identify stripped Ancient ceramic without using Ancient cyan as a dominant Korrathi color.

Materials and surfaces

Scorched steel, cut armor, welded braces, heat-stained barrels, dirty ceramic salvage, exposed conduits, and repurposed industrial clamps. Surfaces may be rough, but mark clusters remain intentional. Do not simulate texture with random noise.

Construction and panel logic

The underlying hull has a crude but workable frame. New modules are visibly grafted through brackets, weld beads, adapter collars, and off-pattern plating. One large stolen component should explain each variant’s combat role. Repeated clan hulls share a chassis but never become perfectly standardized.

Wear and damage

Wear is fresh violence: blast scorch, peeled red paint, bent tips, patched holes, and new weld discoloration. Damage propagates around graft points and overloaded systems. Avoid the bleached, patient decay of Ancient ruins.

Lighting and emissives

Amber power wells and red warning slits pulse unevenly. A stolen Ancient component may retain a small cyan remnant, but it should be occluded, caged, or forced through an amber adapter so ownership remains clear.

Motion and VFX behavior

Thrust is short, turbulent, and slightly asynchronous. Idle engines miss beats; boosts flare hard. Weapon charge telegraphs through heating barrels, venting, and unstable light. Shields, when present, flicker unevenly and fail by sector.

Weapons and VFX verb

Barrels heat with unstable amber light, scorch their housings, and vent unevenly; asymmetrical weapon forms emphasize improvised adapters. A stolen system may cage one cyan remnant inside an amber Korrathi assembly. Reuse and recolor shared modular weapon/FX families; only the approved oversized role graft belongs permanently to a chassis.

Scale and readability

Small hulls read as blades and hooks. Capitals read through one grossly oversized weapon or armored prow, not simply more teeth. Keep asymmetry bold enough to read at gameplay scale; micro-scrap vanishes and is forbidden as a substitute.

Markings

Use broad red hand-painted wedges, kill notches, clan slashes, captured-panel overpaint, and crude hazard blocks. No readable text or uniform navy numbering.

Forbidden traits

  • No immaculate symmetry, parade formation polish, or standardized clean plates.
  • No elegant alien curves, insect hive anatomy, or mystical pirate ornament.
  • No fully understood, seamlessly integrated Ancient module.
  • No random surface noise or teeth on every edge until the silhouette becomes mush.
  • No baked flame, shield, weapon fire, shadow, or destruction.

Production and variant rules

Create a small shared clan-frame kit, then assign one role-defining graft per variant. Preserve common engine and cockpit anatomy while varying captured plates, armor repairs, and large weapons. Each silhouette needs a different threat verb: stalk, cut, latch, ram, tow, command, or besiege.

MVP / Act I production requires the Raider and Marauder only. All other Korrathi assets, including the Raider-Bastion, begin in Act II or later.

Teralith

Design promise

Teralith art presents authority as immaculate engineering. Their licensing navy is sleek, gold, standardized, and quietly oppressive: every panel inspected, numbered, and replaceable. Their order superficially imitates Ancient panel rhythm to support their inheritance doctrine, but the internal geometry is decisively not Ancient. The Teralith monolith is an Ancient relic under custody, never a Teralith-built object.

Silhouettes and shape vocabulary

  • Exact bilateral symmetry, shield forms, disciplined arrowheads, and nested armor.
  • Rounded pauldron plates protect a rigid central spine.
  • Capitals widen into heraldic shields; support craft retain measured proportions.
  • Nothing looks improvised, under-supported, or accidentally asymmetrical.

Palette

  • hull navy #1B2432 and hull black #14171C;
  • plate mid #2A3242 and outline #08090B;
  • authority gold #C99A3D and highlight #F0CF72;
  • emissive amber #FFB44A and hot core #FFE3A8;
  • pale shield blue #7FC7E5 and hot shield #D3F2F5;
  • cream #D8D2BD, markings and seals only.

The faction reads on value contrast. The hull is matte navy falling to black in shadow, and everything that identifies it sits light against that hull. Plate mid separates facets and does not lighten the body. Cream appears only in marking blocks, never as a hull color.

Two separate elements carry the identity and must not merge. Authority gold #C99A3D is matte pigment on plate: chevrons, license bars, rank bands, and inspection seals. Emissive amber #FFB44A is light: one or two uninterrupted identifying lines along the principal chines and nacelles, plus sparse hot bleed at cut panel seams near vents and weapon recesses. Painted gold never glows. Emissive amber is never a paint fill. Gold identifies licensed authority, not wealth decoration.

Restraint is the point. A hull outlined in glowing contours reads as generic neon and destroys the hierarchy that line placement is supposed to carry. Line count does not scale with rank: a Sovereign does not wear more lines than a Sentry, it wears longer and better-placed ones.

Pale shield blue marks command, shield, and legal-service distinctions. It reads hot against a dark hull. It is a functional color that separates shields from the faction’s amber identity lighting, and it is not part of the identity read itself.

Teralith and Civilian local police share a navy family and must not collapse into each other. Police wear a lighter desaturated navy #35506A with large white/cream recognition panels and small amber warning lights, a high-visibility civil livery. Teralith sit several values darker, carry no cream panels, and mark in gold. Value and gold-versus-cream are the tell at any zoom.

Materials and surfaces

Matte faceted armor plate over dark anodized structure, enamel identity blocks, dark sealed joints, polished weapon housings, and clean shield emitters. Surfaces stay low-gloss so the emissive strips and gold markings hold every highlight. Specular hits are narrow and consistent. There are no exposed cables or raw welds on serviceable craft.

Construction and panel logic

Nested plates follow standardized replacement zones. Every panel has a reason, index position, and mirrored counterpart. Seam rhythm may echo Ancient examples, but Teralith panels are shallower, more armored, and organized around inspection access rather than Ancient service buses. Interior ribs, sockets, and fasteners must not match Ancient anatomy.

Wear and damage

Normal fleet assets show minimal wear: polished leading edges, tiny shield scorch, and exact-color replacement panels. Combat damage is localized and quickly boxed off by clean emergency shutters. Derelict Teralith assets may exist later, but should still show disciplined compartmental construction.

Lighting and emissives

One or two amber identifying lines run uninterrupted along the principal chines or nacelles and hold steady. Hot amber bleeds sparsely at cut panel seams near vents and weapon recesses, never as a general edge glow. Pale-blue shields and gold status strips illuminate in exact sequences. All paired lights match. Warning states use measured amber blocks, not Korrathi flicker or Ancient cyan service buses. A broken or unpaired line reads as damage, so undamaged hulls never show one.

Motion and VFX behavior

Engines ignite simultaneously and maintain clean, narrow exhaust. Ships turn in disciplined arcs; formation effects should reinforce shared timing. Shields appear as stable geometric sectors. Weapons telegraph through precise apertures and retract or close when inactive.

Weapons and VFX verb

Pale geometric beams and shield sectors begin at apertures that open and close in measured sequence; their narrow white-hot cores use controlled pale halos. Paired weapon lights and charges always match. Shared modular weapon and FX families may be recolored into this disciplined presentation; do not author a faction-exclusive effect sheet for every Teralith hull.

Scale and readability

At small scale, a single uninterrupted amber line on a dark mass identifies the faction. An emissive line survives downscaling and a painted chevron does not, so scouts and fighters rely on the line alone while capitals carry the line and legible painted markings. At capital scale, nested shield plates and perfect symmetry dominate. Do not add more gold until class is readable; change proportion and armor mass first.

Markings

Use panel numbers as abstract blocks, license bars, rank bands, inspection seals, and mirrored formation marks. Avoid readable lettering. Command blue appears on Sovereign or Warden assets, not every patrol hull.

Forbidden traits

  • No improvised welds, exposed cabling, missing service panels, or random repairs.
  • No actual Ancient internal ribs or visual proof that the doctrine is true.
  • No Korrathi hooks, Civilian cargo clutter, or Blackwater biomorphic form. Curvature is not the prohibition: rounded pauldron plates and shield-arc silhouettes are Teralith. Flowing, grown, or creature-like hulls are not.

Production and variant rules

Unbound

Design promise

The Unbound are biological machine accidents: the Melding, in which flesh and machinery cannot agree where one ends and the other begins. They are not an alien species that deliberately grows ships, and they are not a coherent biotech navy. Their forms preserve enough recognizable engineered structure to make the fusion wrong. Internal production may state the flesh-machine and plague truth; art labels and player-facing copy must not identify a human origin or the Fold-Plague before the reveal.

Hull origin (locked)

Unbound ships are other factions’ hulls, fucked up. There is no native Unbound ship line. Every combat hull, station nest, and drone starts as a victim chassis — militia, Korrathi/scavenger, independent industrial, civilian, or Ancient/Continuity — then a Melding event at one or more system boundaries (engine, interface, power bus, weapon, structural break). Growth is primarily seeding wrecks and derelicts (and high-value live capture under the SALVAGEABLE branch): offspring / process-nodes take hold in empty machine and rewrite it over time. The player should still read “that was a militia escort” or “that was a Korrathi frigate” before they read “monster.”

Silhouettes and shape vocabulary

  • Asymmetrical swollen masses distort recognizable ribs, hull wedges, and apertures.
  • One side may retain engineered geometry while the other flexes, sags, or blooms.
  • Tendon-like tension follows real load paths; growth does not become random tentacles.
  • Mouth-like openings are rare and functional. The primary read is violated machine, not monster face.
  • Prefer remixing existing faction silhouettes over inventing new house shapes.

Palette

  • calcified ivory #D2C7AF and bone highlight #EEE1C6;
  • bruised purple biomass #704A85 and deep tissue #35243F;
  • retained victim metal, led by machine charcoal #24282C but preserving enough of the source faction’s material ramp to identify the chassis;
  • bright sky-blue plasma #35D8FF and hot conduit core #B8F5FF.

Ivory, purple, victim metal, and sky-blue plasma carry the read. Red may survive only in original warning lamps, markings, or weapon FX; it is never the colour of Unbound circulation.

Materials and surfaces

Retained ceramic and structural metal sit beside purple biomass, calcified ivory reinforcement, translucent membranes, muscle bands, and plasma conduit tubes.

Conduits are hardware, not anatomy. Each one is a sealed glass tube of constant bore carrying sky-blue plasma, socketed at both ends into machined metal fittings and strapped to structure by clamps. Tubes bend through a small number of deliberate angles and arcs, run alone or in parallel bundles, and read as retro tube technology bolted through the wreck. The glass wall stays visible where the plasma is dim, so an unlit tube is still an object. Flesh grips, bridges, or replaces machinery; it is not a decorative skin laid over a complete ship. Bone-like growth follows load paths and braces damaged frames rather than reading as ornamental carapace.

Construction and panel logic

Begin from a plausible victim chassis or machine structure. Choose specific system boundaries where the Melding took hold: engine, cockpit/interface, power bus, weapon, or structural break. Preserve some original seams and ribs so engulfment can be read. Cables enter muscle, membranes seal broken panels, and bone-like growth may carry loads. Sky-blue conduit tubes must visibly enter or bridge real engines, interfaces, power buses, weapons, and structural breaks. They are functioning hardware, not surface decoration. There is no modular faction standard.

Conduit runs are routed, never grown. A tube keeps its diameter along its whole length and terminates in a fitting at both ends. It does not branch into capillary webs, dendrites, root systems, or lightning trees, and it never tapers away into hairlines. Where several systems need feeding, run several tubes from a manifold block. Branching morphology reads as vasculature and puts the circulation inside the flesh; the Unbound keep their power supply on the outside, in glass, where it can be seen and broken.

Wear and damage

Old machine wear coexists with active biological repair: bleached ceramic beside fresh membrane, scar tissue around a cracked rib, and a live plasma conduit crossing a dead cable run. Combat damage exposes biological anatomy and broken machinery. Conduit tubes shatter rather than tear, leaving jagged glass in a socket that still grips. Severed runs leak luminous plasma, arc across nearby metal, sputter under load, and leave everything downstream of the break dark. Avoid blood, constant dripping, gratuitous organs, or gore so dense that structural storytelling disappears.

Lighting and emissives

Sky-blue conduit tubes combine circulation, control signalling, and power transfer. They travel along specific paths through membranes and exposed machine buses, then brighten or pulse when the connected system acts. Light must feel routed and conducted, never decorative. The glow belongs to plasma inside a tube. A restrained cyan reflection falls on the metal, bone, or membrane immediately beside a run, and that contact light is what makes the tube read as luminous rather than painted on. It stops there. Cyan never travels through the hull as veins and never opens into a broad neon wash. Some machine lights may fire late or in the wrong order as a plasma impulse crosses them.

Motion and VFX behavior

Idle forms breathe or contract slowly. Local twitches interrupt that rhythm. Engines resemble contracting organs around recognizable nozzles; exhaust fires in peristaltic pulses. Turns may lag on the engulfed side. Weapon telegraphs propagate as a conduit pulse, muscle tightening, or pressure swelling before the separate shot FX.

Weapons and VFX verb

A plasma-conduit path telegraphs pressure or muscle contraction before a separate shot FX leaves the authored hardpoint. Fire should feel involuntary and uneven, never like a clean parade beam; conduit light is the system telegraph, not a permanent glow wrapped around every projectile. Shared modular weapon/FX families may be distorted, tinted, and timed for the Unbound; do not bake a unique projectile into each body or collapse weapon colour into the circulation language.

Scale and readability

At gameplay scale preserve the victim chassis, one major deformation, and the main plasma-conduit path. On larger assets add engulfed bays and repeated ribs, not more tentacles. Every asset must remain readable as a ship/station with a front, back, pivot, and functional anchors. Horror may unsettle orientation but cannot erase gameplay orientation.

Markings

The Unbound add no deliberate insignia. Remnants of victim markings may survive, stretched, occluded, or interrupted by tissue. Do not use repeated hive sigils.

Forbidden traits

  • No deliberately grown alien fleet, elegant biotech civilization, or coherent navy.
  • No generic insect hive, swarm carapace, or derivative bug silhouette language.
  • No gore blanket, tentacle quota, or face on every object.
  • No blood-red circulation, blood spray, or gore-first read; damaged anatomy leaks bright plasma and loses conduit light.
  • No branching conduit morphology. Capillary webs, dendrites, root systems, veins under membrane, and lightning trees all read as vasculature and are forbidden. Power travels in glass tubes of constant bore that terminate in fittings.
  • No perfect symmetry or clean separation between organic and machine regions.
  • No player-facing human-origin or Fold-Plague disclosure before the reveal.
  • No baked exhaust, shield, weapon fire, shadow, or destruction.

Each asset starts from a victim-function brief, then selects one dominant Melding event. Preserve recognizable engineered structure in every design. Canon and design promise

Blackwater is humanity’s autonomous commercial and logistics automation, continuously refabricated across eons after its operators vanished. Production checklist

Before any asset is considered ready for integration:

  • [ ] Produce silhouette thumbnails and identify faction/role from black shape alone.
  • [ ] Lock the faction palette and material cel ramps; record any approved exception.
  • [ ] Make a concept review plate on black, including clean, wear, and scale comparisons.
  • [ ] Archive layered source and export a clean transparent base with stable pivot.
  • [ ] Separate engines, shields, weapons, warning lights, and destruction from the base.
  • [ ] Assign the correct authored wear tier; never improvise wear in wiring.
  • [ ] Provide combat damage and destruction logic appropriate to the material anatomy.
  • [ ] Deliver an anchor sheet for pivots, engines, hardpoints, bays, docks, tools, shields, detachable pieces, and special interaction points.
  • [ ] Run the actual-gameplay-footprint readability test and mixed-fleet comparison.
  • [ ] Confirm current data schema can wire the intended base/body/overlay combination; if not, mark art ready and wiring blocked rather than flattening the asset.
  • [ ] Capture the integrated asset in game under concepts/ and obtain owner visual sign-off before shipping any presentation change.
  • [ ] For any gameplay-visible adoption, update the relevant player-voiced assets/codex/*.md article in the same change and preserve reveal gating.
